I. Introduction to Special Senses
Overview · Nervous System · The 5 Senses
Key Concepts
▼
  • The state of our nervous system determines what we perceive.
  • During sympathetic activation, we experience heightened awareness — we may hear sounds that would normally escape notice.
  • When concentrating on a difficult problem, we may remain unaware of relatively loud noises.
  • The five special senses are: olfaction, gustation, vision, equilibrium, and hearing.
🧠 Mnemonic
"Old Gorillas Vomit Every Harvest" → Olfaction, Gustation, Vision, Equilibrium, Hearing
🫁
II. Olfaction (Smell)
Olfactory Organs · Receptors · Pathways
High YieldPathway
▼
  • Olfactory organs are located in the nasal cavity on either side of the nasal septum.
  • The olfactory epithelium contains olfactory receptors, supporting cells, and basal (stem) cells.
  • Surfaces are coated with secretions from olfactory glands.
  • Olfactory receptors are highly modified neurons.
  • Reception involves detecting dissolved chemicals interacting with odorant-binding proteins.
  • Pathway: Axons → cribriform plate of ethmoid bone → olfactory bulbs (1st synapse) → olfactory tract → olfactory cortex, hypothalamus, limbic system.
  • Unique feature: Olfactory information reaches the brain WITHOUT first synapsing in the thalamus — the only sense to do this!
⭐ Key Concept
Olfactory stimuli have powerful effects on mood and behavior because they directly access the limbic system (emotion center) without thalamic relay.
🧠 Mnemonic
"Smell Goes Direct" — Olfaction is the only sense that bypasses the thalamus and goes directly to the cortex.
👅
III. Gustation (Taste)
Taste Buds · Papillae · 6 Taste Sensations
High Yield
▼
  • Taste (gustatory) receptors are clustered in taste buds, associated with lingual papillae on the tongue.
  • Three types of lingual papillae:
    • Filiform — provide friction, NO taste buds
    • Fungiform — ~5 taste buds each
    • Circumvallate — up to 100 taste buds each
  • Each taste bud has basal cells (stem cells) and gustatory cells with taste hairs extending through a taste pore.
  • Gustatory cells survive only about 10 days before replacement.
  • 6 Primary tastes: sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami (meats/parmesan), and water (pharynx receptors).
  • Dissolved chemicals bind to receptor proteins → neurotransmitter release → action potentials in afferent fibers.
  • Pathway: Cranial nerves → medulla oblongata → thalamus → primary sensory cortex.
  • PTC (phenylthiocarbamide): ~70% of Caucasians can taste it, 30% cannot — genetically determined.
🧠 Mnemonic – Papillae
"Fun Circles Fill, Filiform Fails" — Fungiform and Circumvallate have taste buds, Filiform does NOT.
⭐ Key Concept
Gustatory sensations are strongest when integrated with olfactory sensations — this is why food tastes bland when you have a stuffy nose!

IV. Vision — Anatomy of the Eye
3 Tunics · Retina · Chambers · Lens
High YieldMust Know
▼
  • 3 Eye Layers (Tunics):
    • Fibrous tunic — sclera (white) + cornea (transparent)
    • Vascular tunic (uvea) — iris, ciliary body, choroid
    • Neural tunic (retina) — photoreceptors
  • Iris contains muscle fibers controlling pupil size:
    • Papillary constrictor — decreases pupil diameter
    • Papillary dilator — enlarges pupil
  • Conjunctiva covers inner eyelids (palpebral) and anterior eye (ocular). Conjunctivitis = pinkeye.
  • Lacrimal apparatus produces tears; lacrimal gland contains lysozyme (antibacterial enzyme).
  • Retina contains two photoreceptors:
    • Rods — no color discrimination, highly sensitive to light; dominate periphery; black-and-white in dim light
    • Cones — color vision; densely packed in fovea/macula lutea; high resolution in bright light
  • Optic disc = blind spot (no photoreceptors; where optic nerve exits).
  • Chambers: posterior cavity (vitreous chamber) filled with vitreous body; anterior cavity (anterior + posterior chambers) filled with aqueous humor.
  • Canal of Schlemm drains aqueous humor. Blockage → glaucoma (increased intraocular pressure).
  • Lens contains crystallins — transparent proteins providing clarity and focusing power.
  • Accommodation — lens changes shape to focus on near objects. Normal visual acuity = 20/20.
🧠 Mnemonic – Eye Tunics
"Fans Vow to Never Argue" → Fibrous, Vascular, Neural (retina) = 3 tunics of the eye
⭐ Light Path Through the Eye
Conjunctiva → Cornea → Anterior cavity → Lens → Posterior cavity → Neural retina → Photoreceptors

IV. Vision — Physiology & Pathways
Rhodopsin · Color Vision · Visual Pathway
High YieldPathway
▼
  • Visual pigments are derivatives of rhodopsin (opsin + retinal, which is synthesized from Vitamin A).
  • Night blindness = Vitamin A deficiency.
  • Color vision depends on red, green, and blue cones. Color blindness = inability to detect certain colors.
  • Visual pathway: Photoreceptors → bipolar cells → ganglion cells → optic nerve → optic chiasm → visual cortex (occipital lobe).
  • At the optic chiasm: left visual field → right occipital lobe; right visual field → left occipital lobe.
  • M cells (magnocells) — monitor rods; provide general form, motion, shadows in dim light.
  • P cells (parvocells) — monitor cones; provide fine detail, edges, color.
  • Depth perception = comparing object positions between left and right eye images.
🧠 Mnemonic – Night Blindness
"No Vitamin A = No Rhodopsin = No Night Vision" — Vitamin A makes retinal → retinal + opsin = rhodopsin → rod function

V. Equilibrium
Vestibular Complex · Hair Cells · Semicircular Ducts
High Yield
▼
  • Equilibrium & hearing are provided by the inner ear receptors.
  • Membranous labyrinth contains endolymph; surrounded by bony labyrinth (vestibule, semicircular canals, cochlea).
  • Vestibule → encloses saccule & utricle → sense gravity and linear acceleration.
  • Semicircular ducts → stimulated by rotation of the head.
  • Basic inner ear receptors are hair cells (80-100 stereocilia + 1 kinocilium each).
  • External forces push stereocilia/kinocilium — hair cells do NOT actively move them.
  • Maculae in saccule/utricle contain otoliths (statoconia/mineral crystals in matrix) that shift with gravity.
  • Pathway: Vestibular receptors → vestibular ganglia → vestibular branch of CN VIII → vestibular nuclei (4 functions: integration, relay to cerebellum, relay to cortex, motor commands).
🧠 Mnemonic – What Each Structure Senses
Semicircular = Spinning (rotation)
Utricle/Saccule = Up-down & linear (gravity)
Cochlea = Communication (sound/hearing)

V. Hearing
Ear Anatomy · Ossicles · 6-Step Hearing Process
High YieldMust Know
▼
  • External ear: auricle (pinna) → external acoustic canal → tympanic membrane (eardrum). Ceruminous glands produce cerumen (wax).
  • Middle ear (tympanic cavity): communicates with nasopharynx via auditory (Eustachian) tube (equalizes pressure). Contains 3 ossicles:
    • Malleus (hammer) → Incus (anvil) → Stapes (stirrup)
  • Inner ear: cochlea contains cochlear duct (organ of Corti) — the hearing organ. Basilar membrane separates cochlear duct from tympanic duct.
  • Sound frequency = pitch (Hz = cycles/sec). Sound energy = decibels.
  • 6-Step Hearing Process:
    1. Sound waves → tympanic membrane
    2. Tympanic membrane vibrates → ossicles move (amplification!)
    3. Stapes at oval window → pressure waves in vestibular duct perilymph
    4. Pressure waves distort basilar membrane (frequency → position on membrane)
    5. Basilar membrane vibrates → hair cells against tectorial membrane → stereocilia displaced → ion channels open → depolarization → neurotransmitter release
    6. CN VIII (cochlear branch) → cochlear nuclei of medulla oblongata → brain
  • Frequency → determined by which part of cochlear duct is stimulated.
  • Intensity (volume) → determined by how many hair cells are stimulated.
🧠 Mnemonic – Ossicles
"My Interesting Stereo" → Malleus, Incus, Stapes (in order from tympanic membrane inward)
⭐ Key Concept
Balance and hearing use the same basic receptor type (hair cells). The anatomical location determines what stimulates them: gravity (utricle/saccule), rotation (semicircular ducts), or sound pressure (cochlea).
