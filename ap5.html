Subject overview

What this chapter covers

The integumentary system is the body system made up of the skin and its accessory structures. It is the largest body system, accounting for about 16 percent of body weight and covering about 1.5 to 2 square meters of area. This lesson focuses on how the skin is organized, how it protects the body, how it interacts with blood vessels and sensory receptors, and how it repairs itself after injury.

Core organization
  • Cutaneous membrane: the skin, made of the epidermis and dermis.
  • Epidermis: outer superficial epithelium made of epithelial tissue.
  • Dermis: inner connective tissue layer containing vessels, nerves, and accessory structures.
  • Accessory structures: hair, nails, sebaceous glands, sweat glands, mammary glands, and ceruminous glands.
  • Hypodermis: subcutaneous layer below the dermis, also called superficial fascia.
Functions of skin and subcutaneous layer
  • Protects against shock, abrasion, fluid loss, and chemical attack.
  • Excretes salts, water, and organic wastes through glands.
  • Maintains body temperature through insulation and sweat evaporation.
  • Synthesizes vitamin D3, later converted to calcitriol for calcium regulation.
  • Stores lipids in the subcutaneous layer.
  • Detects touch, pressure, pain, and temperature.

Topic 1

The Epidermis

The epidermis is an avascular stratified squamous epithelium. Its most abundant cells are keratinocytes, which contain the protein keratin. Thin skin has four epidermal layers, while thick skin on the palms and soles has five layers.

Study notes: epidermal layers
  1. Stratum germinativum or basale: deepest layer; contains basal stem cells and melanocytes; forms epidermal ridges.
  2. Stratum spinosum: spiny layer; cells produced by division of stem cells; 8 to 10 layers of keratinocytes.
  3. Stratum granulosum: grainy layer; cells stop dividing, produce keratin, dehydrate, and die.
  4. Stratum lucidum: clear layer found only in thick skin.
  5. Stratum corneum: outer horn layer; 15 to 30 layers of keratinized cells.
Study notes: skin color and vitamin D
  • Skin color depends on pigment and circulation.
  • Carotene is an orange-yellow pigment that accumulates in epidermal cells and fatty dermal tissues.
  • Melanin is produced by melanocytes and protects against ultraviolet radiation.
  • Skin color depends on the rate of melanin production, not the number of melanocytes.
  • Heat-related vessel dilation can redden skin; reduced blood flow can make skin pale; severe oxygen reduction can cause cyanosis.
  • With ultraviolet radiation, epidermal cells convert a steroid into vitamin D3, which the liver and kidneys convert into calcitriol.
Important process: Keratinization forms a layer of dead, protective cells filled with keratin. It takes about 15 to 30 days for a cell to move from the stratum germinativum to the stratum corneum.

Discussion and reflection

Explain how the epidermis can be protective even though its surface cells are dead.

The epidermis protects the body through keratinization. As keratinocytes move upward from the stratum germinativum, they produce keratin, dehydrate, and die. The stratum corneum is therefore made of dead, keratinized cells, but this makes it tough, water resistant, and protective against abrasion and fluid loss.
Topic 2

The Dermis and Subcutaneous Layer

The dermis lies between the epidermis and subcutaneous layer. It contains connective tissue, vessels, nerves, and many accessory structures. Beneath it, the hypodermis stabilizes skin while allowing movement.

Study notes: dermal organization
  • Papillary layer: outer dermal layer made of areolar tissue; contains smaller capillaries, lymphatics, sensory neurons, and dermal papillae.
  • Reticular layer: deeper layer made of dense irregular connective tissue; contains larger blood vessels, lymph vessels, nerve fibers, collagen, and elastic fibers.
  • Dermal papillae project between epidermal ridges, increasing surface area and strengthening attachment.
  • Hair follicles and sweat glands extend into the dermis.
Study notes: hypodermis
  • Also called the subcutaneous layer or superficial fascia.
  • Made of elastic areolar and adipose tissues.
  • Connected to the reticular layer by interwoven connective tissue fibers.
  • Stabilizes skin position but permits separate movement.
  • Has few capillaries and no vital organs, making it a site for subcutaneous injections.

Discussion and reflection

Compare the papillary layer, reticular layer, and hypodermis in terms of tissue type and function.

The papillary layer is the outer dermal layer of areolar tissue and includes smaller vessels, lymphatics, sensory neurons, and dermal papillae. The reticular layer is deeper and made of dense irregular connective tissue with larger vessels, nerves, collagen, and elastic fibers. The hypodermis is not part of the skin itself; it is a subcutaneous layer of areolar and adipose tissue that stores lipids, stabilizes skin, and allows movement.
Topic 3

Accessory Structures

Accessory structures include hair, hair follicles, glands, and nails. These structures originate from the epidermis during development, are located in the dermis, and project through the epidermis to the surface.

Hair and follicles
  • Hair covers most of the body except palms, soles, lips, and parts of the external sex organs.
  • Hair protects, insulates, guards openings, and detects very light touch.
  • Each hair follicle has a sebaceous gland that lubricates the hair.
  • Hair is keratinized as it is produced.
  • Hair color is determined genetically and produced by melanocytes at the hair papilla.
Glands
  • Sebaceous glands: holocrine oil glands that secrete sebum to lubricate, protect, and inhibit bacteria.
  • Apocrine sweat glands: found in armpits, around nipples, and groin; produce sticky secretions associated with odors.
  • Merocrine sweat glands: widely distributed; coiled tubular glands that discharge onto the skin for evaporative cooling.
  • Ceruminous glands: produce cerumen, or earwax.
  • Mammary glands: produce milk.
Nails
  • Fingernails and toenails protect delicate digit tips.
  • Nails are dead cells packed with keratin.
  • Nail production occurs in a deep epidermal fold near the bone called the nail root.

Discussion and reflection

How do accessory structures support protection, temperature control, and sensory function?

Hair protects and insulates, guards openings such as the nose, and is sensitive to light touch. Sebaceous glands secrete sebum, which lubricates and protects the epidermis and inhibits bacteria. Merocrine sweat glands cool the skin by evaporation, excrete water and electrolytes, and flush microorganisms or chemicals from the surface. Nails protect the delicate tips of digits.
Topic 4

Response of the Integument to Injury

Because skin contains many active germinative cells, it can respond rapidly to changes in health, activity, and localized injury. Repair involves inflammation, scab formation, migration of cells, granulation tissue, scar formation, and strengthening.

Study notes: four steps in repair
  1. Bleeding and inflammation: bleeding occurs and mast cells trigger inflammation by releasing histamine.
  2. Scab and granulation tissue: blood clots form a scab; germinative cells migrate around the wound; macrophages clean the area; fibroblasts and endothelial cells divide, producing granulation tissue.
  3. Scar tissue: fibroblasts replace damaged tissues with a fibrous repair called scar tissue; inflammation decreases and the clot begins to disintegrate.
  4. Strengthening and keloid: fibroblasts continue strengthening the area until a raised section of scar tissue, called a keloid, forms.

Discussion and reflection

Why are inflammation, macrophages, fibroblasts, and scab formation all necessary for repair?

Inflammation begins the repair response and helps recruit cells to the wound. The scab stabilizes and protects the area. Macrophages clean the wound by removing debris. Fibroblasts move in, divide, and produce the fibrous repair tissue that becomes scar tissue. Together, these steps protect the wound, remove damaged material, rebuild tissue, and strengthen the repair.
