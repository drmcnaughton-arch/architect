System Overview

The skeletal system serves several critical functions. It is composed of bones, cartilage, ligaments, and stabilizing connective tissues.

5 Primary Functions

  • Support: Structural framework for the body.
  • Storage: Minerals (calcium) and lipids (yellow marrow).
  • Blood Cell Production: Red marrow site.
  • Protection: Shields delicate internal organs.
  • Leverage: Facilitates force of motion.

Bone Anatomy

Bones are classified by shape, which dictates their function and location.

Classification of Bone Shapes

  • Long bones: Long/thin (Arms, legs, fingers).
  • Flat bones: Thin/parallel surfaces (Skull, ribs, scapula).
  • Sutural bones: Small, irregular (Between skull flat bones).
  • Irregular bones: Complex shapes (Vertebrae, pelvic).
  • Short bones: Small/thick (Ankle, wrist).
  • Sesamoid bones: Develop inside tendons (Near joints).

Physiology, Repair & Aging

Fracture Repair Steps

  1. Hematoma: Bleeding creates a clot, cells die.
  2. Calluses: Internal and external calluses stabilize the break.
  3. Spongy Bone: Osteoblasts replace cartilage with bone.
  4. Remodeling: Osteoblasts/osteocytes reduce calluses over time.

Aging

Bone mass reduction (osteopenia) often starts between ages 30-40. Osteoporosis is severe loss impairing function.

Discussion

Question: Why is fracture repair a multi-step process involving cartilage and bone cells rather than immediate regeneration?

Answer: The body must first stabilize the area with a clot and callus to prevent further damage, then replace the temporary structural scaffolding (cartilage) with stronger bone tissue to restore full function.
