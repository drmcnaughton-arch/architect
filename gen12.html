Overview

DNA stores genetic information as a linear sequence of deoxyribonucleotides. One DNA strand can serve as a template for transcription into messenger RNA. The mRNA is then read by ribosomes during translation, using triplet codons to specify amino acids in a protein.

Core idea: Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. This lesson explains how the genetic code is organized, how scientists deciphered codons, and how transcription creates RNA from DNA templates.

Genetic Code Characteristics

The genetic code is written in mRNA as three-ribonucleotide words called codons. Each codon is read in a specific reading frame during translation.

Study Notes

A

Triplet code

  • Each codon contains three ribonucleotide bases.
  • Codons are read from mRNA during translation.
  • Each codon corresponds to an amino acid or a translation signal.
B

Unambiguous and degenerate

  • Unambiguous means one codon specifies only one amino acid.
  • Degenerate means one amino acid can be specified by more than one codon.
  • Degeneracy helps explain how 64 triplets can encode 20 amino acids plus signals.
C

Reading frame

  • The code is commaless once translation begins.
  • The code is nonoverlapping: one mRNA ribonucleotide belongs to only one codon in a given reading frame.
  • Insertions or deletions can shift the reading frame and alter downstream codons.
Nearly universal: Viruses, prokaryotes, archaea, and eukaryotes generally use the same coding dictionary, although important exceptions exist.

Discussion and Reflection

Reflection Prompt

Why does a single-nucleotide insertion usually cause a more disruptive frameshift than a three-nucleotide insertion?

Model Answer

A single-nucleotide insertion changes how every downstream triplet is grouped, so the reading frame shifts and many codons after the insertion are altered. A three-nucleotide insertion adds one extra codon, but because codons are read in groups of three, the original reading frame can be reestablished after the inserted triplet.

Deciphering the Genetic Code

Early studies used mutations and synthetic RNAs to determine how nucleotide triplets correspond to amino acids.

Study Notes

A

Frameshift evidence

  • Sydney Brenner proposed that the code was a triplet code.
  • Francis Crick's phage T4 studies used insertions and deletions to show that frameshifts alter downstream codons.
  • Triplet-based recovery after three insertions or deletions supported the triplet model.
B

Synthetic RNA systems

  • Polynucleotide phosphorylase can produce synthetic RNAs.
  • Artificial mRNAs were translated in cell-free systems containing ribosomes, tRNAs, amino acids, and other factors.
  • Radioactively tagged amino acids helped identify which amino acid was incorporated into a polypeptide.
C

Assays and copolymers

  • Homopolymers used one ribonucleotide type, such as poly-U.
  • Mixed heteropolymers tested base composition, but not exact sequence.
  • The triplet binding assay linked a single codon to a charged tRNA and its amino acid.
  • Repeating copolymers generated repeating triplets for additional assignments.
Key example: Poly-U caused phenylalanine incorporation at very high levels, supporting the assignment of UUU to phenylalanine.

Discussion and Reflection

Reflection Prompt

Why did mixed heteropolymers help decipher codons, but still leave an important limitation?

Model Answer

Mixed heteropolymers helped because researchers could control the proportions of different ribonucleotides and compare the expected triplet compositions with the amino acids incorporated into polypeptides. The limitation was that this approach revealed likely base composition, not the exact sequence of each triplet. A codon with one A and two C bases could be ACC, CAC, or CCA, so more precise methods were needed.

Coding Dictionary, Wobble, and Universality

The coding dictionary assigns functions to 64 possible triplets. Most triplets encode amino acids, while three act as termination signals.

Study Notes

A

Start and stop signals

  • AUG encodes methionine and initiates most polypeptide chains.
  • In bacteria, the initial methionine is a modified form called N-formylmethionine.
  • UAA, UAG, and UGA are termination codons and do not encode amino acids.
  • A mutation that creates a stop codon within a gene is a nonsense mutation.
B

Wobble and order

  • The first two bases of a codon are often more critical than the third.
  • Wobble allows relaxed base pairing at the third codon position.
  • Chemically similar amino acids often share one or two middle bases in their codons.
  • This ordered pattern may buffer some mutations.
C

Universality and exceptions

  • The genetic code is nearly universal across major groups of organisms.
  • Bacteriophage MS2 studies helped confirm the genetic code through sequencing of gene products.
  • Mitochondrial DNA and some organisms show exceptions, such as UGA encoding tryptophan in human and yeast mitochondria.

Discussion and Reflection

Reflection Prompt

How can the code be both nonoverlapping and still allow overlapping genes?

Model Answer

For any one reading frame, the genetic code is nonoverlapping: each ribonucleotide is part of only one codon. Overlapping genes occur when a single mRNA has different initiation points, causing translation to begin in different reading frames. Each individual frame is read nonoverlappingly, but different frames can use the same RNA sequence in different groupings.

Transcription and RNA Polymerase

Transcription synthesizes RNA on a DNA template. RNA polymerase directs this process and, in bacteria, uses a sigma factor to recognize promoters during initiation.

Study Notes

A

Template and coding strands

  • The template strand is transcribed into RNA.
  • The complementary DNA strand is called the coding strand.
  • The RNA transcript is single-stranded and is complementary to the DNA template.
B

RNA polymerase

  • RNA polymerase synthesizes RNA using DNA as a template.
  • It uses ribonucleotide substrates.
  • No primer is required for initiation.
  • In E. coli, the holoenzyme includes a sigma factor for promoter recognition.
C

Promoters and termination

  • Promoters are DNA sequences upstream of the transcription start site.
  • E. coli promoters commonly include consensus sequences near the -35 and -10 regions.
  • The -10 region is called the Pribnow box.
  • Termination can involve RNA hairpin formation or rho factor.
Cis and trans: Cis-acting DNA elements are nearby DNA sequences on the same molecule. Trans-acting factors are molecules that bind cis elements to influence gene expression.

Bacterial Transcription Stages

1. Initiation

RNA polymerase holoenzyme binds the promoter. The DNA helix unwinds near the transcription start site so the template strand is accessible.

2. Elongation

The sigma factor dissociates. The core enzyme moves along the DNA template while the growing RNA transcript extends from its 5 prime end toward its 3 prime end.

3. Termination

RNA polymerase reaches a termination sequence. In bacteria, the transcript may fold into a hairpin, or termination may require rho factor.

Discussion and Reflection

Reflection Prompt

Why is promoter recognition important for regulating transcription efficiency?

Model Answer

Promoters determine where RNA polymerase binds and where transcription begins. The interaction between promoter sequences and RNA polymerase affects how easily initiation occurs. Stronger promoter recognition can make transcription more efficient, while weaker recognition can reduce RNA production. In bacteria, the sigma factor is especially important because it helps RNA polymerase recognize promoter sequences.

Eukaryotic Transcription and RNA Processing

Eukaryotic transcription occurs in the nucleus, involves multiple RNA polymerases and transcription factors, and produces primary transcripts that must be processed before translation.

Study Notes

A

Eukaryotic transcription

  • Occurs within the nucleus.
  • mRNA must leave the nucleus for translation.
  • Chromatin must uncoil or remodel to make DNA accessible.
  • General transcription factors help RNA polymerase bind DNA.
  • Enhancers and silencers regulate transcription.
B

RNA polymerases

  • RNA polymerase I transcribes rRNA in the nucleolus.
  • RNA polymerase II transcribes mRNA and snRNA in the nucleoplasm.
  • RNA polymerase III transcribes 5S rRNA and tRNA in the nucleoplasm.
  • RNA polymerase II is highly regulated for protein-coding genes.
C

RNA processing

  • Primary transcripts become mature RNA through processing.
  • A 5 prime cap protects the transcript and helps it function.
  • A poly-A tail is added at the 3 prime end after cleavage near the AAUAAA signal.
  • Introns are removed and exons are joined by splicing.
Introns and exons: Introns are intervening sequences removed during RNA processing. Exons are retained and represented in the mature mRNA.

Splicing and Editing

Alternative splicing

Different mature mRNAs can be produced from the same pre-mRNA by removing introns and joining exons in different combinations.

Spliceosome

Pre-mRNA introns are removed by spliceosomes. Small nuclear ribonucleoproteins, called snRNPs, help form the spliceosome and guide excision.

RNA editing

Posttranscriptional RNA editing can insert or delete nucleotides, or substitute one base identity for another, modifying the final transcript.

Discussion and Reflection

Reflection Prompt

Why does eukaryotic gene expression require more processing and regulation than bacterial transcription?

Model Answer

Eukaryotic DNA is packaged in chromatin, so DNA must become accessible before transcription can occur. Eukaryotes also use multiple RNA polymerases, general transcription factors, core promoters, enhancers, and silencers to regulate transcription on a gene-by-gene basis. After transcription, pre-mRNAs must be processed with a 5 prime cap, poly-A tail, and intron removal before mature mRNA can leave the nucleus and be translated.
