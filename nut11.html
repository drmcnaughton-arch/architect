The Fat-Soluble Vitamins

💧
Absorption & Transport
  • Require bile for digestion and absorption
  • Travel through the lymphatic system
  • Carried by transport proteins in the bloodstream
  • Chylomicrons transport them after intestinal absorption
⚠️
Storage & Toxicity Risk
  • Stored in liver and adipose (fat) tissues
  • Not readily excreted — accumulate over time
  • Increases risk of toxicity with excess intake
  • Deficiency develops slowly due to stored reserves
⚠️ Key Contrast
Unlike water-soluble vitamins, excess cannot be excreted in urine — making supplement overdose genuinely dangerous.

A
Vitamin A & Beta-Carotene
Objective 11.1 — Roles, deficiency, toxicity, and food sources
👁️
Vision
  • Maintains clear cornea
  • Converts light energy to nerve impulses in the retina
  • Photosensitive cells contain rhodopsin — requires retinal
  • Repeated small losses of retinal require constant replenishment
🧬
Cell Differentiation & Reproduction
  • Epithelial cells form skin and line body surfaces
  • Mucous membranes line the inside of the body
  • Goblet cells synthesize and secrete mucus
  • Retinoic acid regulates cell differentiation, growth, and embryonic development
  • Supports sperm development and normal fetal development
  • Beta-carotene functions as an antioxidant
📉
Deficiency
  • Large problem in developing countries
  • Night blindness → total blindness
  • Increased risk of infectious diseases
  • Can result in death
  • Worsened by poor protein status
📈
Toxicity
  • Binding proteins become saturated
  • Free vitamin A damages cells
  • Children most vulnerable → bone defects
  • Risk from animal sources, supplements, fortified foods
  • No effect on acne
🥕
Food Sources
  • Liver — highest animal source
  • Dark leafy greens, deep orange/yellow produce
  • Golden rice and biofortified foods
  • Measured in RAE (Retinol Activity Equivalents)
Forms of Vitamin A (Retinoids)
Retinol — active in vision  ·  Retinal — supports reproduction; major transport/storage form  ·  Retinoic acid — regulates cell differentiation & embryonic development  ·  Beta-carotene — provitamin A; antioxidant

💬 Discussion Question — Vitamin A
Why is vitamin A deficiency particularly devastating in developing countries, and what two-pronged approach does it take — both through direct effects and through protein status?
📝 Your Thoughts
✅ Answer
Vitamin A deficiency is a major problem in developing countries, where it is a leading cause of preventable blindness in children and dramatically increases risk of death from infectious diseases. The deficiency progresses from night blindness to total blindness as rhodopsin cannot be replenished without retinal.

There is also a nutritional catch: vitamin A requires transport proteins to move through the bloodstream. In populations with inadequate protein intake, there are fewer transport proteins — meaning even when some vitamin A is consumed, it cannot be effectively utilized. This protein-vitamin A interaction compounds the deficiency in food-insecure regions. Additionally, vitamin A supports epithelial integrity, so deficiency weakens the body's first line of defense against pathogens.
D
Vitamin D
Objective 11.2 — The sunshine vitamin; hormone-like roles, deficiency, and toxicity
☀️
Roles in the Body
  • Active form functions as a hormone
  • Essential for making and maintaining bones
  • Assists absorption of calcium and phosphorus
  • Protects against cognitive decline; slows Parkinson's progression
  • Regulates adipose cells — may influence obesity development
  • Signals immune cells to defend against infection
  • May protect against heart disease, type 2 diabetes, some cancers
🦴
Deficiency Conditions
  • ~10% of Americans are deficient
  • Rickets — children; bones fail to calcify; can affect >50% of children in some countries
  • Osteomalacia — adults; soft, flexible, brittle, deformed bones
  • Osteoporosis — fractures; calcium loss from bones; especially elderly
  • Deficiency causes secondary calcium deficiency
Contributing Factors to Deficiency
Dark skin · breastfeeding without supplementation · lack of sunlight exposure · not consuming fortified milk
⚠️
Toxicity
  • Most likely of all vitamins to cause toxicity
  • Raises blood calcium → kidney stones in soft tissues
  • May harden blood vessels; can cause death
  • Risk from high-dose single supplements
  • Vitamin D3 more effective than D2
🔬
Synthesis & Sources
  • Synthesized in skin from sunlight (UV-B)
  • Activated in liver then kidneys
  • Fortified milk, fortified cereals, fatty fish
  • Elderly have reduced ability to activate vitamin D

💬 Discussion Question — Vitamin D
In what ways does vitamin D protect against disease beyond just bone health? Consider multiple body systems.
📝 Your Thoughts
✅ Answer
In the brain and nerve cells, vitamin D protects against cognitive decline and slows the progression of Parkinson's disease. In muscle cells, it encourages growth in children and preserves strength in adults. Vitamin D signals immune system cells to defend against infectious diseases, and may regulate adipose tissue cells in ways that influence the development of obesity.

In many cases, vitamin D enhances or suppresses the activity of genes that regulate cell growth. Recent research suggests vitamin D may protect against heart disease, type 2 diabetes, inflammation, brain disorders, macular degeneration, hypertension, and some cancers.
E
Vitamin E
Objective 11.3 — The body's primary fat-soluble antioxidant
🛡️
Antioxidant Role
  • Two subgroups: tocopherols and tocotrienols
  • Each has alpha, beta, gamma, and delta forms
  • Only alpha-tocopherol is maintained in the human body
  • Stops the chain reaction of free radicals
  • Protects cells and their membranes
  • Protects LDLs from oxidation → may reduce heart disease risk
🍃
Recommendations & Sources
  • RDA based on alpha-tocopherol form only
  • Widespread in foods; primary source = vegetable oils
  • Fresh foods preferred — destroyed by oxidation and heat
  • Also found in nuts, seeds, fortified cereals
💡 Tip
Because vitamin E is destroyed by heat and oxidation, raw or lightly cooked foods are significantly better sources than highly processed ones.
📉
Deficiency
  • Primary deficiency is rare
  • Secondary deficiency from fat malabsorption
  • Red blood cells break open → erythrocyte hemolysis
  • Causes neuromuscular dysfunction
📈
Toxicity
  • Liver regulates vitamin E; toxicity rare
  • UL is 65 times greater than RDA for adults
  • Extremely high doses may interfere with vitamin K activity
  • Can cause hemorrhage at very high supplemental doses

The chain reaction that vitamin E stops as an antioxidant
The deficiency symptom where red blood cells rupture
Common cause of secondary vitamin E deficiency
✅ Answers
  1. Only maintained form → Alpha-tocopherol
  2. Chain reaction stopped by vitamin E → Free radicals
  3. Red blood cells rupture → Erythrocyte hemolysis
  4. Cause of secondary deficiency → Fat malabsorption
💬 Discussion Question — Vitamin E
How does vitamin E's role as an antioxidant potentially reduce the risk of heart disease? What is the mechanism, and why might fresh food sources be preferable to supplements?
📝 Your Thoughts
✅ Answer
Vitamin E acts as the body's primary fat-soluble antioxidant. Its key role in heart disease prevention relates to LDL cholesterol: when LDL is oxidized by free radicals, it becomes more likely to contribute to arterial plaque buildup. Vitamin E stops the chain reaction of free radical oxidation, protecting LDL particles and cell membranes from damage.

Fresh food sources are generally preferred over supplements because vitamin E is easily destroyed by heat and oxidation during processing. Additionally, whole foods provide the full spectrum of tocopherol and tocotrienol forms, whereas supplements typically supply only alpha-tocopherol. Very high supplemental doses also carry risk: they can interfere with vitamin K activity, potentially leading to impaired blood clotting and hemorrhage.
K
Vitamin K
Objective 11.4 — Blood clotting, bone health, and gut bacteria
🩸
Roles in the Body
  • Primary action: blood clotting — activates prothrombin
  • Metabolism of bone proteins — activates osteocalcin
  • Low vitamin K linked to low bone density
Key Proteins
Prothrombin — clotting factor activated by vitamin K
Osteocalcin — bone protein requiring vitamin K for activation
🌿
Food Sources & Non-Food Sources
  • Leafy greens — kale, spinach (richest sources)
  • Fruits — avocado, kiwi
  • Vegetable oils
  • Synthesized by intestinal bacteria — but insufficient to meet needs
  • Bioavailability from gut bacteria is limited
📉
Deficiency
  • Primary: inadequate dietary intake — rare
  • Secondary: antibiotics kill vitamin K–producing gut bacteria
  • Failure of bile production impairs absorption
  • Newborns at risk — sterile gut, no bacteria → vitamin K injection at birth
📈
Toxicity & Drug Interactions
  • Toxicity not common; no UL established
  • No adverse effects with high dietary intakes
  • High doses can reduce effectiveness of anticoagulant drugs (e.g., warfarin)
  • Patients on warfarin must maintain consistent vitamin K intake

✅ Answers

  2. Bone protein → Osteocalcin
  3. Non-food synthesis source → Intestinal bacteria

💬 Discussion Question — Vitamin K
What are the non-food sources of vitamin K, and are they sufficient to meet the body's needs? What food sources are most reliable, and why must patients on anticoagulants be careful?
📝 Your Thoughts
✅ Answer
Non-food sources: Vitamin K is synthesized by intestinal bacteria in the gut. However, the amount produced is insufficient to meet the body's needs, and bioavailability from this source is limited — so diet remains essential.

Food sources: The richest sources are leafy greens such as kale and spinach. Fruits like avocado and kiwi, as well as vegetable oils, also contribute. Newborns are especially at risk because their gut is sterile at birth, which is why a vitamin K injection is given at delivery.

Drug interaction: Patients on warfarin (an anticoagulant) must be careful because vitamin K directly opposes warfarin's mechanism. Warfarin works by blocking vitamin K's role in activating clotting factors; high vitamin K intake can therefore reduce warfarin's effectiveness. These patients are advised to keep vitamin K consumption consistent rather than eliminating it entirely.
